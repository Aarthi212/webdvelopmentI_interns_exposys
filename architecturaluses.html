Architectural Uses




Architectural terracotta is an ancient form of masonry that is used extensively even till today. Indian terracotta manufacturers used to hand press, pour, and double-mould the clay mix. Plaster casts have been found around several ancient sites in Afghanistan, Bangladesh, India and Pakistan. Bhitargaon temple and the Jain temple in the Mahbubnagar district are some examples of ancient terracotta use in architecture. Because of the similarities in motifs and manufacturing process, scholars have noted cross cultural pollination between the Hellenic and Indus River Valley sculptural terracotta traditions.

Chinese, Korean, and Japanese terracotta-making traditions were focused on non-architectural uses such as statuary or cookware but various forms of terracotta tiles were popular roofing materials. During the 1860s in England and the 1870s in America, architects began using unglazed terracotta to decorate the exterior surfaces of buildings. It was used on a number of buildings in Birmingham; on the elaborate terracotta facade of the Natural History Museum, in London; the Victoria and Albert Museum (1859–71); and the Royal Albert Hall (1867–71). Earlier, in 1842-45, St Stephen and All Martyrs’ Church, at Lever Bridge in Bolton had been built almost entirely from terracotta. Curiously, terracotta received approval as a building material from the Arts and Crafts movement, since it was deemed to be a handmade material, designed by craftsmen. In America, the Chicago School of architecture was an early convert to terracotta. The architect Louis Sullivan (1856-1924), for instance, was noted for his elaborate glazed terracotta decorations, that would have been extremely difficult to produce in any other medium. Fired clay was also used by Chicago designers Daniel Hudson Burnham (1846-1912) and John Root (1850-91) in the curtain walls of their Reliance Building (1895), and by William Le Baron Jenney who pioneered its use in skyscrapers as a way of reducing the risk of fire.

Terracotta can be used both on the exteriors and interiors either structurally or non-structurally in the construction of chimneys, bricks, copings, planters, water pipes, roofing tiles, shingles, architectural details and other ornaments. It has poor resistance to tension and low shear strength but is strong in compression. The performance of terracotta in affected by its porosity. It can fail due to a number of reasons like poor manufacturing or installation (can be due to improper loading, or the mortar being used being too strong, which puts stress to the terracotta block), weathering, atmospheric pollution which causes salt formation, freeze-thaw cycling etc. By the 1920s, a process known as mechanised extrusion was capable of mass-producing terracotta blocks in standard form for roofing, flooring and cladding. Although it couldn’t compete with the modern building materials like concrete and steel. Minimalism and Modernist architecture too, caused its declined use throughout the 1930’s because of the changing aesthetic preferences.

The individual but repetitive nature of the fabrication process made terracotta construction much more economical than carved stone for elaborate building exteriors. Terracotta bricks are an essential part of the majestic structures like the Great Chinese Wall, the Haghia Sophia, or the dome of the Cathedral in Florence, Italy. Therefore, their use in modern architecture is a revival of this age old building technique. It does not come as a surprise that architects who prefer green architecture perceive terracotta as the ideal material for facades in public buildings since it does not require maintenance, it’s ecological, inexpensive, sustainable and offers great potential in terms of design.

The School of Dancing Arches by Samira Rathod Design Associates in Gujarat, India, too, is a notable example of terracotta use in architecture as the school building with the lopsided vaulted roofs in the shape of a child’s doodles is made entirely of terracotta. “The design of the new school grew organically as a series of classrooms that dance their way through the trees, encountering alcoves, cracks and crevices, projections, niches, inhabited bridges, boxes and mezzanines,” said the architects. The buildings of the school are surrounded completely the tobacco fields near the town of Bhadran in Gujarat where the school is located. Another great example of terracotta being used in architecture is seen at these apartment blocks in Ho Chi Minh City by the Sanuki Daisuke Architects, that have planted courtyards in their centre. The Sanuki Daisuke Architects came up with a screen of decorative terracotta clockwork in the front of these buildings, due to the relatively low cost of terracotta as a decorative material used in architecture. These blocks are perforated and hence give natural ventilation and cooling by shading over the fronts of these buildings, while still maintaining privacy from the streets in the front.
