Terracotta in the World

Terracotta is prevalent in history across so many genres because it can be found everywhere and is known as being the most commonly found clay across every continent. The earliest reference made to terracotta was in prehistoric art, with artefacts being found as far back as 24,000 BC. These early pieces were found to be Palaeolithic terracotta figurines, rather than cooking vessels. Paleolithic terracotta figures were fired in primitive kilns, created underneath open fires.


The oldest recorded piece of terracotta in the world is the The Venus of Dolni Vestonice (c.26,000 BCE). With a height of approximately 4.5 inches and width of 1.7 inches it was discovered at a Stone Age settlement in the Moravian basin, near Brno, in the Czech Republic. It now resides in the Vienna Natural History Museum. It was baked in an earthen oven at nearly 700 degrees Celsius made from a combination of locally sourced clay and powdered bone. Famous terracotta figurines from the era of Neolithic art include: The Enthroned Goddess Figurine (c.6,000 BCE) from Catalhuyuk, Anatolia, and The Thinker of Cernavoda (5000 BCE) from the lower Danube region in Romania.


Early Egyptian, Minoan, Mycenean, Greek and Etruscan cultures, from around the Mediterranean, all employed terracotta for making figurines. It was widely used by sculptors during the era of Hellenistic art (323-30 BCE), in particular. It was also used in Early Christian art, for tomb reliefs (from c.200 CE).


In 3000 BCE, during the Early Bronze Age Small terra-cotta figures were found in Greece, and larger objects dating from the 7th century BCE were also found. The craft was carried to Etruria by Greek artists, which further evolved in Rome. In Greece, terracotta statuary was used extensively to decorate temples. The modelled Etruscan statues, at times very Greek in style but often more fierce, were admired widely in antiquity. Figures on Etruscan sarcophagi often were of terra-cotta. The findings of Roman Terracotta statues are scarce.


Venus of Dolni Vestonice (c. 26, 000 BCE)

Molded statuettes 6 to 7 inches (15 to 18 cm) in height are common throughout the ancient world. Cypriot figures often comprise groups of dancers or warriors, and the Cretan feature lively poses of women, horsemen, or animals. Post 7th century BCE, the subjects of the figures became more or less mundane, such as a nurse with child, a teacher and pupils, an actor in costume. The Tanagra figurines, found in Tanagra in central Greece, Boeotia, are the best known of this kind. In the Hellenistic period, from the 4th century BCE, centres of statuette production moved to Asia Minor and westward, being found throughout the Roman Empire as far as Britain. Styles in the East became more ornate and influenced by Oriental values in design and subject.


The set of fired clay figures known as the Chinese Terracotta Army - made during the period of Qin Dynasty art (221-206 BCE) are one of the finest works ever created in the history of sculpture. Around 7000 life- sized ceramic clay warriors were individually crafted, accompanied by generals and horses, equipped with real weapons, and installed in battle formation in three underground pits. These figures were unearthed in 1974 adjacent to the tomb of the First Qin Emperor, Qin Shi Huangdi, in Shaanxi province, China. The figures were commissioned by the Emperor in 247 BCE. Work started in about 246 BCE and is estimated to have taken almost 40 years to complete, with a manpower of 700,000 workers. The role of the army was to serve the Emperor in the afterlife. The collapse of the Roman Empire (c.450) led to a dramatic decline in the use of terracotta. Early Renaissance helped in the revival of terracotta as an artistic medium. Renaissance sculptors, Donatello and Lorenzo Ghiberti rediscovered the ability of terracotta for creating images of Christian art, it was a discovery that came about through their close knowledge of bronze sculpture - the use of clay is central to the creation of bronze statues.


Terracotta Warriors Exhibit at The World Museum

To recreate devotional images and other figures, which were then shot, painted and gilded, Clay was moulded to produce a low-cost alternative to more costly materials, such as marble and bronze. The use of glazed terracotta for relief sculpture and church altarpiece painting was popularised by other artists, including the Della Robbia family. For instance, see the pulpit reliefs by the Florentine artist Benedetto da Maiano for Santa Croce in Florence (1481).


Terracotta was also used in Renaissance portrait painting, as exemplified by Antonio de ‘Benintendi’s wonderful Bust of Cardinal Giovanni de’ Medici (later Pope Leo X) (c.1512, Victoria and Albert Museum). Many sculptors have often used terracotta models when sending designs or making experiments for larger sculptures or for paintings.


In short, terracotta was re-established by Renaissance sculpture as a major medium of artistic expression and innovation. The tradition was further developed by Bernini (1598-80) and Alessandro Algardi (1598-1654) during the era of Baroque sculpture, particularly in the area of relief sculptures and portrait bust. Terracotta was explored for its decorative characteristics during the 18th century, while the great Antonio Canova (1757-1822) continued to use it for models, until he replaced it with plaster in the early 19th century.
