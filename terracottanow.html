Terracotta Now

Terracotta has made its mark from prehistoric ages and is one of the oldest art forms that are still prevalent in the modern era. It has been used by people across the globe for centuries.
From the magnificent terracotta army in China to the beautiful figurines found from the ruins of ancient Rome, it has played an essential role in the development of art through the ages. Traditionally, it is seen as a mystical material due to its combination of four of the five vital elements- air, Earth, fire and water.
Terracotta products have proven to be so well loved by people in both the past and the present. Today, terracotta is still used in pottery and art for the home and beyond. Its superior durability, vibrant colours , and easy to mould nature make it a popular choice for sculptors and architects. It is a surprisingly versatile material and can complement a wide range of interiors. The colour, in a contemporary setting is used to add warmth to the modern and monochromatic themes. Terracotta is also used as a color word, to denote the color of earth. As terracotta literally means baked earth the distinct color comes from the iron content in clay reacting with oxygen during firing, which creates the rich, earthy tone. The color itself has some variation, from more red tones, to pale orange, to darker browns, and is composed of 89% red, 45% green and 36% blue, with 70% saturation. Some pantones shades of terracotta are: PANTONE 16 - 1526 TPX Terra Cotta- This is a traditional terracotta shade.


PANTONE 17 - 1540 TPX Apricot Brandy- This shade is darker than the Terra Cotta shade listed above. However, its tone is very similar.


PANTONE 16 - 1720 TPX Strawberry Ice- A more pink toned shade that reflects the rosier tones sometimes found in terracotta pottery.
PANTONE 14 – 1313 TPX Rose Cloud- A warm beige with pink undertones that’s ideal for a paler look of terracotta.
PANTONE 18 - 1438 TPX Marsala – Colour of the Year 2015- A darker shade from the rusty red family, reminiscent of terracotta tones and known for its popularity as Colour of the Year.


Clay Cups - The utility and advantage of terracotta as a medium can be seen prominently in its inclusion in industrial and commercial applications. A recent example of this is the large scale implementation of terracotta products to be put into use in the Indian railways. India has had a longstanding tradition of using earthen pots for a variety of uses but as of late, that has decreased significantly due to the use of plastic products, but these to have their drawbacks. Due to this the Indian railways have implemented the use of ‘Kulhads’, a small earthen cup. Kulhads are small earthen cups used to serve a number of traditional delicacies such as mishti doi , mallaiyo and tea. The cups are also used in a single use format, which perfectly suits the need in replacement of single use plastic cups which are in use as of now for the majority of drinks given out at railway stations, primarily tea. The cup itself is cheaper to produce compared to other materials, biodegradable and also inherently hygienic due to them being fired in a kiln. It is also known that the cups itself add an enhancement to the flavour of the beverage due to the earthly aroma imparted by the clay.

As of now roughly 400 railway stations are using this system with the plan to implement them nation wide to all 7000 stations, thereby massively cutting down on single use plastics. In addition to drastically reducing toxic plastic use there will also be large amounts of employment generated especially in the craft sector due to this project. The number is astronomically large, pre pandemic figures show India at roughly 23 million commuters by train per day. With estimations of employment for upto 2 million of the craft and pottery industry. Though it isn’t just smooth sailing for a venture like this, especially one of this size.
Firstly with the pottery itself, handmade pottery is never uniform, in its shape and size. And clay in different parts of the country are vastly diverse from one another, making it near impossible to standardise it like an industrial process. Coordinating artisans from across the country to work, and to gather as well is a mammoth task, also providing artisans with the required equipment and raw materials is difficult currently. Hopefully these difficulties will be navigated efficiently as a project such as this is massive benefit to everyone involved especially the artisans while also greatly combating the problem of pollution and single use plastics.
