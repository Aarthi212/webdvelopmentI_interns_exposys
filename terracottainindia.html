Terracotta in India

Terracotta has a traditional value in India. It has been used for centuries and has taken shape over thousands and thousands of years. Terracotta uses the combination of air, earth, fire and water which are the five vital elements, thus is seen as a mystical material. Terracotta is used in the field of art and pottery. Due to its extensive background, it’s no wonder that India has some prominent when it comes to the usage of terracotta. Most famously, the largest terracotta sculpture ever made was the Ayyanar horse - created in Tamil Nadu. What makes the end product unique from region to region in India, is the difference in clay type and colour, as well as the sensibilities of the artist, varied culture, religious practices, and traditions of the region.


Temple at Bishnapur, West Bengal

West Bengal is well known for Terracotta art. Some of the well known towns for Terracotta art are Murshidabad, Jessore, Birbhaum, Digha, and Hooghly. The art form came to this state in the 16th century with the influence of Vaishnavite movement which found expression in Terracotta sculpting on Krishna temples built by them. Some of the most exquisite patterns of temple terracotta panels are made there. Rural areas of the state are a treasure trove of finely crafted terracotta pots and also figurines including those of beautiful horses, along with other items, small and large, of practical use as well as decorative. From being used for daily use and home décor items, terracotta came to be used for making temples in 15th – 16th century AD in Bengal. The popularity of this building material lay in its universal and abundant availability across regions. Once people learned to bake the clay, they had a way of giving permanence to the items created by them. Slowly, terracotta art was put to more ambitious uses, such as, creating buildings, sometimes replacing stone and wood carvings, due to their non-availability.


Delhi holds on to its tradition of ‘Blue’ Pottery for which it is very well known. The colour blue is derived from Persian blue dye which is added to the clay to get that beautiful, exquisite colour. Hence, Delhi’s glazed blue pottery is considered to be a very impressive, tough and pleasing form of Indian terracotta pottery.

Odisha Terracotta Art dates back to 4th century BC and the tribal artisans create unique designs using special clay which has many takers both within the country and abroad. Animal figurines of bulls, horses and elephants are exquisitely moulded and highlighted, and left in their original clay colour. Other household items like jars, tea cups, plates, roof tiles, pots, toys, candle stands, etc. are also made in Odisha with the unique tribal touch. Jewellery like bangles and necklace sets are also popular items of the state.

Ayanaar Horses, 1938, Tamil Nadu

Tamil Nadu also has a rich cultural heritage and the most common form of Terracotta pottery in Tamil Nadu are those created by the Ayyanar cult. The popular terracotta figures made by them include large sized, realistic looking figures of elephants and horses, etc. In fact, villages of the state follow a tradition of having a huge terracotta horse figure guarding its entrance. This horse is a companion of Ayyanar, the Tamil Village God. The common belief is that the Tamil deities ride them and hence, they are also seen as protectors of the village and those who travel through. Apart from religious figurines, daily use items like water pots, cooking pots, grain storage containers, etc. are also made using Terracotta in the state. As per legends, the potters of Tamil Nadu, also known as Kuyavar or Velar, trace their origin to the heavenly master craftsman Vishwakarma.


Jammu and Kashmir terracotta art has two distinctive forms. While one, made in Ladakh, consists of icons, statues, and images related to Buddhism, and made mainly to cater to the various monasteries, the other consists of tea kettles, barley wine pots, kitchen stoves, oil lamps, etc. These items are finely moulded, brightly coloured and used for practical purposes, as well as home decor. The kettles especially are beautifully crafted with its spout, handle, lid as well as brazier.

Bastar in Madhya Pradesh has a rich tradition of creating terracotta figures for rituals as well as for day to day uses. The tribals of Bastar create intricate life-like figurines of Hindu deities, human forms, birds, horses, snakes and huge elephants.

Molela Terracotta Art

Rajasthan also has an age-old tradition of terracotta art and the state is well known for its various distinctive terracotta articles. The arid state also has a special connection with clay and terracotta as people in villages carry water from distant sources to their homes in terracotta pots. Moreover, their daily worship at home is connected to terracotta idols of deities.Alwar in Rajasthan is famous for making paper-thin light-weight pottery, also known as Kagzi, while Pokharan is known for red and white terracotta articles with geometrical patterns. Terracotta horse figures, Ganesha idols, as well as idols of local deities like Nag Dev or the serpent god, Bhairav etc are popular in the state. Bikaner district is famous for colourful pottery items painted with lac colours. Terracotta toys and animal figurines are sold in village haats or markets as well as in fairs.


Bihar Terracotta Art goes back to the Mauryan period (2nd-3rd century BCE). Horses are a recurrent theme in Indian terracotta across the various states of India and Bihar is no exception. Darbhanga in Bihar is well known for its terracotta horses that are painted in bright rainbow colours on completion. Other items include clay elephants that are placed on roof tops to signify marriage in the house.

Hand Painted Pots, Gujarat

Gujarat is famous for terracotta products made using the wheel, especially ones made by the artists from Gundiyali in the Bhuj district. Exquisite hand-painted clay pots with geometrical patterns, almost identical to the ones excavated from the sites of the Indus Valley Civilization. The womenfolk spend their time in decorating the earthen wares.

Haryana has a tradition of terracotta art which dates back to Harappan culture. Today, the terracotta artists from the state create products such as colourfully painted vases, pots, lamps, toys, human and animal figurines, wall-hangings, and musical instruments among others. Beautiful terracotta jewellery including neck and ear pieces are a specialty of the state. Traditional hukkah and smoking pipes are also very famous.
