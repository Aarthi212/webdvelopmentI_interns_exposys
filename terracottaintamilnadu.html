Terracotta in Tamil Nadu

Tamil Nadu is famous for its magnificent stone temples. The giant butter ball of Mahabalipuram, the Cholas at Thanjavur, and the huge temple of Goddess Meenakshi in Madurai, to name a few. However, there is a less known fact about Ayyanar, as these gems are hidden away in remote villages. Across Tamil Nadu, terracotta is famous, but it is recognised more culturally and traditionally.


Terracotta Horses of Tamil Nadu

Each village is guarded at the entrance by an enormous terracotta horse, the horse of Ayyanar. Ayannar is a folk God who rides a white horse and protects the villages from evil spirits, the gramdevta of the village and its protector against all evils. Ayyanar has an enormous moustache ,big teeth and wide open eyes. Veerans are the commanders who surround Ayyanar at the entrance. In Tamil Nadu, potters are known as Kuyavar, Kulalaar or Velar and they trace their origin to Vishwakarma, the divine craftsman himself.


Kritimukha

Ayyanar horses range from less than a meter to our 6 m in height. They are painstakingly made by mixing moist clay with straw and sand for a proper consistency. First the legs are made using four clay cylinders which are rolled out with a piece of wood. Then the body is built up gradually. After that, the accessories like bells, mirrors, grotesque faces (kirtimukha) and crocodiles (magar), are made separately as is the head. Then the parts are joined together on the auspicious 10th day, when the figure of Ayyanar is seated on the horse and given its features. This huge terracotta figure then is baked in a rustic kiln of straw and verati (dried cow dung) covered with mud. Some parts of the larger figures have to be filed separately, joined together and fired again. Sometimes the faces are painted red to denote anger and the neck is painted blue to denote calm The rest of the body is decorated in bright colours. The specialty of these horse is that their tail, their ears, the head and their four legs can be removed separately.


A copper alloy figure of Aiyanar
Ayyanar (also spelt Aiyyanar, Ayanar or Iyenar) is a Hindu village god of Tamil Nadu also worshipped by Sri Lankan Tamils. He is primarily worshipped as a guardian deity who protects the rural villages. Most officiating priests are derive from local potter community, but other caste members also officiate in his temples.The temples of Aiyanar are usually flanked by gigantic and colorful statues of him and his companions riding horses or elephants. There are number of theories as to the origins of the deity as well as the etymology of the name. He is associated with the god Ayyanayake by the Sinhalese people of Sri Lanka. (Source: Wikipedia)

Pondicherry (now, Puducherry) is a coastal town situated in Tamil Nadu known for its French history and ambience. People here have been making handcrafted items for more than 5000 years. Although the origins of Terracotta craft are a mystery, excavations at Arikamedu revealed how the toys surpassed in beauty than those of Satavahanas.


The regional specialty lies with both the glazed and unglazed pottery, which is famous in the whole of the country. The most important raw material, clay has been used for ages in Puducherry to make various items like utensils, jewellery and other decorative items. Over the time this craft has grown more advanced.


These terracotta products are mainly produced by a community called “Kulalar” predominantly found in and around Villianur commune Panchayat of Puducherry. The knowledge of this craft has been passed down for generations.

Small stones and other unwanted materials are removed in the first step. Then, Green clay 20% ,Thennal 40% , fine sand (savudu) 40% are mixed thoroughly and kept for some time for curing. Later, a small portion of the mixed material is taken and attached with adjacent places as per the product to be developed. Time consumed to make the product is based on the size and detailing work planned on the product.


Moulds are made if there are lots of orders. The mould is made only for the parts of body or figure, the ornamentation and the motives are done by hand, and the product is fully completed. It is set to dry in the shade for 12 hours for curing. Then, to improve the durability of the produced product, it is kept under the fire for half an hour. Materials used for firing the statue are, Poda Husk ( Vaikol particles), Wooden powder, Saanam. After the firing process, the hot ware is covered with sand to cool.


The specialty of the Villianur Terracotta works is the proper proportion of the figure and the motifs used on the product. Designed according to the generation and the consumer needs. They are known for the minute works on the idols or products. Even custom orders are made. Only natural colours are used for the process.
